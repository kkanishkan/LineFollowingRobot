Line Following Robot

Christopher Wu & Kanishkan Kukarajah

Purpose:

The purpose of the lab was to create a line following robot (vehicle). It was to be able to follow a specifically designed course on the floor and complete it with relative ease. It was created using specific components; a breadboard, a series of chips wired to two sensors to detect whether or not the robot is following the marked path correctly. The chips were able to decide which wheels needed to be turned and when, so a turn could be made when necessary. The robot was meant to follow a black line.

Theory of Operation (Description of the circuit):

(A dark/black line on a white reflective surface): Using the two reflective sensors mounted below the breadboard, the colors/contents of the surface were detected. The sensor uses an LED, that uses its reflection to decide whether or not the surface is black or not. This is due to the fact that black absorbs light and cannot reflect it, using this information the sensor can determine whether or not it is on a black surface.

When the sensor is on a black coloured surface, it makes pin 1 to close the circuit, thus the current is closed and it does not proceed. The current travels down the breadboard based on the method used to set up the circuit and chip being used (The SN74LS245 or PICAXE 28x1). So normally if the left sensor detects black, it causes the right motor to turn on. So in turn, if the right sensor is the one detecting black, it will cause the left motor to turn on. Finally, if the sensor detects its own reflection it means the surface is no longer black but whiter. If this were to occur, the both sensors would turn off, in turn causing both motors to also turn off. This usually takes place at one sensor at a time, so the robot could carefully adjust its course (it will turn the sensors on and off until the robot is back on course – it’s turning slowly). This explanation of the circuit function is concurrent with the SN74LS2405 chip used alongside the SN74LS40 inverter.

(A white reflective line on a dark/black surface): If the purpose of the robot was to follow a white line rather than black line the surface it is tested on must be black or extremely dark. Again the sensor with the LED within, it again uses the reflection to determine the colour of the surface, to determine whether it is white or not. A white line will cause a reflection of the LED within the sensor and return a signal that allows the designated motor to move forward. If the left sensor happens to detect a white line, it will begin turning the right motor. Now if the right sensor happens to detect a white line, it will begin turning the left motor. Now if the sensors were to detect black, they would stop the motors from turning by closing the circuit. This usually happens once at each sensor until the robot has adjusted its course. This is the appropriate circuit analysis for the SN74LS2405 without the use of the inverter.

Procedure:

A.      Testing the Sensor

1.       Review schematic diagram

2.       Identify pin configurations of the sensor and solder the 4 pins to wires

3.       Connect pin 1 of the sensor to a 10k ohm resistor

4.       Connect pin 2 straight to ground

5.       Connect pin 3 to a resistor, preferably low voltage (330 ohm)

6.       Connect pin 4 straight to ground like pin 2

7.       Place an LED with its anode in the same column as pin 1 of the sensor and the 10k ohm resistor

8.       Connect the cathode to ground and supply power through a USB cable

 

B.      Using SN74LS245 Chip and Inverter (74LS04)

1.       Place the 74LS245 chip and Inverter (74LS04) on the breadboard

2.       Connect pins 1 and 20 of the 74LS245 to +5V

3.       Connect pins 10 and 19 of the 74LS245 to ground

4.       Choose the following pins together on the 74LS245

a.       Pins 2 to 5

b.      Pins 6 to 9

c.       Pins 11 to 14

d.      Pins 15 to 18

5.       Determine the negative and positive wires of the motors

6.       Connect the positive of the motor to pin 11 of the 74LS245 and the negative to ground

7.       Complete the above steps once again for the second motor but the positive wire should be connected to pin 18 instead

8.       After determining that the wheels are able to work, attach the motors to the bottom of the breadboard using the glue gun and secured with tape and attach the wheels

9.       Connect the following pins from the 74LS245 to the 74LS04

a.       Pin 6 to pin 2

b.      Pin 2 to 13

10.   Connect the following pins of the 74LS204 individually to a 10k ohm resistor (connected to +5V) and to pin 1 of the reflective sensor as well

a.       Pin 1 (One sensor)

b.      Pin 12 (Other sensor)

11.   Connect pins 2 and 4 of both reflective sensors to ground

12.   Connect pin 3 of both reflective sensors to a 330 ohm resistor that is then connected to +5V

13.   Supply power via USB cable (positive/negative wire to positive/negative terminal on breadboard)

14.   Test the robot on a black line to follow

 

C.      Using the Picaxe 28x1

1.       Place the Picaxe 28x1 in the middle of the breadboard

2.       Connect pin 19 to ground

3.       Connect pin 20 to the positive terminal

4.       Connect a 22k ohm resistor to pin 6 of the Picaxe 28x1

5.       Using a 10k ohm resistor, connect pin 1 to a positive terminal

6.       The ground wire on the serial connector should be in the same column as the 22k ohm resistor

7.       In that same column connect a 10k ohm resistor from the positive terminal as well

8.       The input and output wire of the serial connector should go to pin 8 and 7 respectively of the picaxe

9.       Connect one wire from each motor to a negative terminal

10.   Take three to four output pins and jumper their columns together in order for the current to be combined for the motors

11.   Repeat the above step for another set of output pins for the other motor

12.   Connect the positive wire from the motors to a column that contains the combined output pins of the picaxe

13.   Take two more 10k ohm resistors and place on an empty column no the breadboard

14.   Connect pin 1 of each sensor to the same column as the two 10k ohm resistors just recently placed

15.   Connect those columns to a desired input pin on the picaxe with a wire

16.   Connect two resistors (330 ohms) to the positive terminal, and the other side connected to an empty column.

17.   Connect pin 3 of the sensors to each resistor

18.   Connect pins 2 and 4 of the sensors to ground

19.   Supply power via USB cable and make sure serial connector is properly connected

20.   Using Picaxe Programming editor, write a specific code that uses the inputs of the sensors in order to make a wheel stop or be in motion (subroutines occur based on inputs)

21.   Run the program and test to see if it works

Observations:

There are quite a few observations that were made over the course of this lab. Together with my partner there many problems we overcame. The biggest and main problem that we encountered was the reflective sensors not functions, this may have been to do the poor soldering technique. The sensors were re-soldered and re-tested several times to make sure they were functional. Our next problem was the fact that the breadboard we were originally using was not functional, so we obtained another from the lab instructor and rewired everything once more. We had originally wired the board without the inverter when using the 74LS245N and the sensors were working oppositely of what we wanted them to do. The motors turned off when black was detected and moved when white was detected. This was corrected through the use of the inverted chip (74LS04), this solved the problem and the robot was following the black course outlines along the floor. The final problem we faced during the lab was the fact that PICAXE version of the board was not functioning this was only because some of the pins were wired incorrectly, this was immediately corrected and robot was functioning once more. The VCC and ground were the cause of the problem, there were connected at the wrong pins.

The sensors were controlling the position and direction of the robot. When the left sensor detects its colour (white or black depending on if the sensor was removed or not) it turns the right motor. When the right senor detects the colour it turns the left motor. If neither detects the correct colour it turns the motor off to reorient itself correctly to go along the course (This is explained in the theory of operation).
